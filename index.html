Research Interests

Currently I work on constructing classical solutions to the Einstein equations (of General Relativity), and scalar-tensor modified gravity theories. More broadly though, what really motivates my work is that I am interested in finding surprising results from physical mathematical models. For example, I have investigated how modified gravity theories could break down in ways completely different from what could happen in general relativity. In particular, I have shown that some scalar-tensor theories can dynamically suffer a breakdown in hyperbolicity. Currently I am working on solving a class of scalar-tensor theories that can be motivated by effective field theory arguments. As these theories have solutions that violate the Null Convergence Condition, which lies at the heart of most of the classical theorems of black hole and cosmological dynamics, these theories will likely offer a host of surprising solutions, which we have only just begun to seriously explore with some mathematical rigor! While Einstein's General Relativity has been studied for over 100 years, the theory may still offer surprising results. One particular itch I've been working to scratch is understanding how similar the Einstein equations are to the Navier-Stokes equations of classical fluid dynamics (here the surprise would be: when General Relativity was first formulated, no one thought it would have any similarities with classical fluids; the Einstein equations simply look nothing like the Navier Stokes equations). Some researchers have found results that suggest that gravitational waves could behave in a way similar to a two-dimensional fluid near the surface of a nearly-extremal Kerr black hole. I am working with physicists and mathematicians on devising novel ways to solve the Einstein equations to explore solutions in the nearly-extremal case. For my research I mostly use the tools of numerical relativity and perturbation theory. A great feature of using numerical methods is that one can construct concrete, specific solutions, which could be potentially compared with actual observational data (e.g. I have worked on computing the gravitational and scalar waves produced by two inspiraling black holes in scalar-tensor theories). For a more detailed overview of my past research projects, it's probably best to look at a list of my publications (see the links below).
